Vector Assignment

This project explores how accessible schools are by bicycle in Leiden. Using GIS and isochrone analysis, the map visualizes travel times and school accessibility throughout the city.

Remote Sensing
Sustainability

Project Overview

For this vector assignment, I decided to map the time it takes for the residents of Leiden to bike to schools. I chose Leiden, as it is right near my hometown, and I was interested in seeing how concentrated the schools are. My goal was to visualize how many residents have easy access to schools in Leiden. Having schools nearby housing is really important for both the children and the parents. For the children, it allows them to bike to school themselves, as they get older. It also often means that everyone lives close by, giving children the oppertunity to play at other houses. For the parents, nearby schools means they do not lose too much time dropping their children off at school. The neighbourhood also benefit from a school nearby, as it creates a community, where children can safely grow up.

GIS is used for this project, because it has datasets with all the information ready, ensuring I did not have to do field work to gain my information. Furthermore, GIS creates a clear map, where the isocrones are automatically put in, saving a lot of time.

Methods

To build this map in QGIS, a few key steps were needed. First, the data of all the schools and residences in Leiden had to be downloaded and loaded within GIS, I did this by using the QuickOSM plugin. Then, using Isochrones, I was able to visualize three circles, for 1,2 and 5 minutes of travel times. Small enough for the chosen area and only 3 points, as otherwise the map would get too chaotic. Finally, I styled the layers and overlaid them over the base layer.

  • Data collection: OpenstreetMap
  • Cartographic design choices: To make the travel zones stand out, I chose purple, as it is an unnatural color to appear on the map below and immediately catches the eye. By adjusting the transparency, I made the underlying layer still readable, to clearly show the streets. The streets have to be clearly visible, so that inhabitants of Leiden kan still recognize where they live and how far a nearest school is.
  • Software and tools used: QGIS, OpenStreetMap Data, QuickOSM, ORS Tools

Results & Findings

The final map shows that most of the city has very easy access to schools on their bike, with many purple circles, most even overlapping with other circles. This means that residents are not confined to the choice of a specific school due to travel hurdles. One can conclude that most people within Leiden live within 5 minutes of at least one school, showing that Leiden is a compact and bike-friendly city.

Reflection

For this project, a lot of new skills had to be implemented. First of all, I got in touch with three new plugins: OSM, QuickMapService and Openstreetmap. Using Isochrones, to calculate specific travel times from points chosen. A lesson learned is to not choose a very big area, as QGIS often took very long or crashed, when the file was too big. Using vectors was very interesting, as it works really well and gives you valuable information with the click of a few buttons.

These kinds of maps are really useful for real-life implementation, as municipalities can get valuable information out of them. The map can expose certain areas of the city that have less accessibility to schools, showing municipalities where to build better infrastructure or new schools. Urban planners can use it for many different implications, from schools to hospitals. Using this technique, one can clearly see what certain neighbourhoods need.

Some limitations are that one can not clearly see what each new ring represents, making it more difficult to read the map. Furthermore, I did not filter on the different types of schools. Universities, primary schools and highschools are all mentioned with the same point. This limits the map, as it is not useful for a young family to have a university next to their street. Further mapping with more specifics is needed to draw clear conclusions. Lastly, the dataset did not take all schools into account, due to some schools sharing the building with other companies and therefore not showing up as fully 'school'.

Overall, this map has taught me a lot about working with vectors and isochrones and ended up giving me a really clear map showing just how accessible Leiden is.

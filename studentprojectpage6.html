Vegetation Classification (NDVI)

A vegetation index map of Utrecht, showing the different types of vegetation and types of land use in Utrecht. Further I made vegetation classification maps, also on Utrecht.

GIS Analysis
Remote Sensing
Sustainability

Project Overview

The objective of this map was to analyze environmental patterns through remote sensing and classification of land uses. I focused on vegetation health and the classification of land uses within Utrecht. I classified Utrecht by “training” the software to recognize certain properties as itself. To find the most accurate approach for environmental monitoring, this project compares a human-guided supervised classification against a fully automated unsupervised classification. The classification of a city can be valuable for environmental monitoring, as the program can understand and process large amounts of data, whilst humans would take years.

I, thus, ended with three maps: an NDVI, unsupervised classification map and a supervised classification map.

Methods

First, I took a course by ESRI on Performing Supervised Pixel-Based Image Classification to understand the mechanics of training software. Once I had finished the course, I created a spectral profile for different land cover types. After, I trained the software by showing samples that correspond with specific land covers. I then ran the training samples through GeoWizard, where I got the unsupervised version first. Right after I also ran the supervised file with the training samples I made before. Lastly, I also made an NDVI version, where one can see the health of the vegetation of Utrecht.

  • Data collection: Satellite Imagery
  • Cartographic design choices: Changed the symbology to clearly separate the different land covers by color
  • Software and tools used: ArcGIS

Results & Findings

The results of the unsupervised map are not super accurate; one can see where most buildings are urban or industrial, but the further classification is quite confusing. One can clearly see where the city is and where nature is, but once in nature the classification is not that accurate. Sadly, the same can be said for the supervised map. Even though water is mapped, the river was not taken into account. This was due to the training sample of the wetlands not being accurate and had some water in it as well. This caused the model to think that certain rivers are wetlands. However, in opposition to the unsupervised version, the developed region is much more accurate. The cultivated area is also much more concise and in balance with reality. The same can be said for the forest. The map of the NDVI of Utrecht shows that the city causes bad vegetation, or the lack of it. However, once out of the city, the amount of vegetation can be said to be quite healthy and dense.

Reflection

This was not my favorite map to work on. Mostly, because the training samples went wrong and I could not find a way to retry without having to restart everything. If I had more time or did a map again, I would definitely change this one. I do think training the software and it being quite accurate is cool to have learned. Some other limitations are that I would have exported from ArcGis, instead of making the screenshots I now did, which tempered with the quality of the image.

Such maps are, however, very useful for real-life implications. They can be used by urban planners to look at whether there is enough greenery in the city. Furthermore, classification is useful to monitor biodiversity and habitat, to see whether habitats are shrinking or growing, allowing them to protect wildlife if needed. Overall, a very good skill to have acquired and interesting to train the software. I showcased the “garbage in, garbage out” principle, where if you train the samples wrong, the map comes out inaccurately.

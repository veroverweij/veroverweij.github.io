SDG 5.5 – Women in National Parliaments

For this first project, I made an interactive map of one of the Sustainable Development Goals by the UN.

GIS Analysis
SDG
2026

Project Overview

My map is showing the proportion of seats held by women in national parliaments, which corresponds with goal 5.5: Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls. The indicator is the amount of women being involved in the national parliament, to ensure that women have full and effective participation and equal opportunities for leadership at all levels of decision-making. The goal of this map is to visualize which countries are still behind on the goal gender equality.

By making an interactive map, all data are clearly visible and countries can individually be analyzed.

Methods

To start, I downloaded the data from the UN website for the specific goal I chose: Gender Equality in politics. Then, I exported the file to QGIS and added it to a world map. Using the valuable information from the attribute table, I added the percentage of women in parliament to the countries. Then, I edited the map so that the top 5 countries are highlighted, to showcase the countries that have the highest scores. Lastly, it was all about changing the layout and adding a legend to the map.

  • Data collection: The dataset was from the United Nations
  • Cartographic design choices: For the color scheme I chose green, with the darker the green, the higher the percentage of women in the parliament. The color green represents positive news, thus, showing that women in parliament should be advocated for and is a positive goal to strive for. Furthermore, it is a clear color, with the gradients being easily readable.
  • Software and tools used: QGIS

Results & Findings

Next to the interactive chloropleth map, I also made a simplified chloropleth map. A choropleth map is a thematic map using colors to represent different data of a specific topic. They represent quantitative, enumerated data, relying on visual variation in color. They are often used by governments or authoritative bodies, they are also the default for the UN, when mapping SDGs. An advantage of this type of map is that it is easy to read and to see how different countries score on the specific thing. Clear is that there are still very little countries with at least 50% of the people in parliament being women. With some countries, such as Yemen and Oman even being below the 10%. The countries in South America are doing surprisingly well, with most countries being above 30% in representatives. The map exposes many countries, and the fact that they still have a long way to go to eventually reach gender equality.

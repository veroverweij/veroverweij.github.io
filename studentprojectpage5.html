What is Multi-Criteria Analysis

MCA is a decision-making technique that evaluates multiple factors at the same time to identify optimal locations or solutions. It combines various criteria using weighted analysis to create the corresponding maps. This is important because in real life, there are often more factors than just one that one has to take into account. For example, when selecting sites to build schools, you need to consider environmental impact, safety, and accessibility. Multi-Criteria Analysis allows for a structured way to answer such complex issues. It brings technical, economic, social, and environmental factors into a single process. It is valuable for GIS for many reasons; using multiple factors is one of them. Furthermore, it allows for abstract data to be able to put into use in real life implications. GIS also allows for higher capacity and speed, allowing planners to simulate different scenarios quickly.

There are also some limitations to GIS-MCA, such as the “Garbage in, Garbage out” principle. If bad data is inserted, the spatial model cannot fix that. The system can also be misused or manipulated to favor specific viewpoints.

Methods typically fall into two main categories: value function methods and outranking methods. Value function methods assign scores and weights to different factors, to give every location a final “suitability score”. Outranking methods, on the other hand, compare sites to determine the option that outperforms the others.

Overall, GIS-MCA has become a significant part of GIS, functioning as a decision-support tool. Its quick analyzing and simple outcome are valuable for decision-makers.

Regional modeling of soil sensitivity to water erosion in JIJEL region (Algeria) using MCA and GIS

Water erosion causes severe land degradation in northern Algeria, specifically the region Jijel. The agricultural fields take up around 41% of this region, directly putting the livability of the region into danger. Earlier studies have studied this area, but were limited to specific factors, instead of combining different parameters. Furthermore, there was a lack of data due to land inaccessibility. The researchers therefore used MCA in GIS to qualitatively map out the regional soil vulnerability. MCA was very useful in their case because they used data available and weighted them to make a map, without needing to collect all the specific data in Algeria. The research was done to help the local decision makers, as they did not have access to information on where the erosion was worse. Thus, now, they can define the regions that need the most immediate help.

Criteria

The criteria that were chosen are for the weighted sum criteria and include: precipitation, slope, lithology, and vegetation cover. These were chosen because they represent the four most important factors responsible for water erosion. Precipitation, for example, is the catalyst for water erosion and the Jijel region is also known for its high precipitation. The slope is included as it is the accelerating factor of how fast the water will flow once on the ground. Furthermore, the vegetation cover protects soil, and more vegetation leads to less soil degradation. Lastly, the lithology is used to determine the durability of the ground material, as they all weather differently.

Data Collection

To obtain all the data, multiple datasets were used. For remote sensed data, data from the Shuttle Radar Topography mission is used, as well as the UMT in world geodetic system. Furthermore, Landsat 8 images were used from the US geological survey website, which were mosaiced in ArcGIS and chosen for the winter period. The climate data came from the World Clim website and for the lithological data, a geological map from GETIC 2008 is used. The objective is to obtain a sensitivity map for water erosion based on a weighted sum model. The researchers multiplied cell values in normalized factor maps by a corresponding weight, to then add the weighted values of each map. Results

Climatic Factor:

Strong erosive character including the whole region, very high precipitation is in the center, followed by high precipitation. Moderate and low are not represented, showing the aggressive nature of the rainfall.

Topographic Factor:

Very high slopes over 55 percent of the overall area while high reliefs account for 28%. The very rugged topography gives Jijel its high erosive potential.

Lithology:

The most resistant formations cover the majority of the study area. The most vulnerable are in the west and southeast, with 39%.

Vegetation Cover:

Low to moderate plant cover. The study area soils are more vulnerable to winter rainfall erosivity.

Water Erosion:

Very strong sensitivity to erosion with 77%. These results correspond with very steep slopes and heavy rainfall. The hypothesis was therefore correct, that all factors influence the amount of erosion that happens in the area. Discussion

The difficulties of this study lie in the lack of data quantifying estimates of soil loss. We cannot quantitatively compare the results of this study with others in the study area, due to lack of previous work on water erosion in this area. On the other hand, the water erosion sensitivity map is an important source of information for local decision-makers regarding land degradation and erosion control, protection of forest resources, and especially against land degradation caused by fires. It is furthermore the reason why it is increasingly used in erosion studies and its integration into a GIS environment facilitated processing with the many advantages that this tool provides. In fact, the weighted-sum method for modeling water erosion provides a valuable approach to discriminate against the regional heterogeneity of water erosion sensitivity on the entire study area.

The water erosion sensitivity map is of particular interest to local communities; it allows visualizing the regions most threatened by erosion that require immediate management. Therefore, using MCA showcased how different factors all influence the erosion and map it so it can be used by decision makers.

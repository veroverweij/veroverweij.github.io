Digital Elevation Model

For this map, I made a digital elevation model of the region of the Ooijpolder. This region is characterized for its dikes and risk of flooding, therefore, it is a very interesting region to further analyze how humans have influenced the elevation around the river De Waal.

Project Overview

For my region, I selected the Waal and its surrounding area, as my grandparents live in the Ooijpolder. For my region I chose to put the Ooijpolder and the river in the middle, including a part of the city of Nijmegen and a few other villages. It is a region with beautiful nature, and I thought it would be interesting to see the change in elevation due to the dikes built in the area. The area is known for its flooding and in 1995 the whole polder had to be evacuated, as the authorities were afraid it was going to flood. Therefore, a network of dikes was built to prevent further flooding. Thus, one can easily recognize the dikes within a elevation map, which can help further understanding the situation around the Waal. I did the old map option. As I was interested in how much it would have changed. The map is from 1843, made by W. Kuyk, A Bayly and J.M. Huart. It was a map made of the whole of Gelderland, but I decided to only use a small part of it. The total size of the new merged/clipped DTM and DSM file was 1.4 Gb after merging, and the study area size is around 70 square kilometers.

By mapping this area, experts can further understand where the weaknesses of this area lie. In case of a flood, the elevation can show what areas are in danger of flooding, and thus, where further reinforcement is needed. It is also useful for the main public, as they can get more educated about the danger of floods and what the Netherlands does against it.

Methods

Firstly, I picked my region and downloaded it from OldMapsOnline. After, I downloaded both the DSM and DTM and made a legend for both. Then I made the old map with the overlapping newer version, by corresponding the correct coordinates on both maps and allowing QGIS to render them on top of another. I clipped all the unnecessary parts of the map so I finished with a smaller file. To make the finishing product, I changed the transparency and colors to make the map as clear as possible.

  • Data collection: OldMapsOnline
  • Cartographic design choices: I chose the color blue and this color ramp, because it is easy for the eyes, looks appealing and clearly shows the differences between the shades of blue. It was also easier to make the rivers to be white, because they could then not be confused for land or interfere with the map. I chose to bold a few of the villages in the area, together with Nijmegen, so that it is more clear in what area this map is located. The map is centered around the river so that one can clearly see how the dikes are built around the river in the situation of a flood.
  • Software and tools used: GIS

Results & Findings

In this first map, one can see the digital surface map, where the buildings are not taken into account. In the map one can clearly see that Nijmegen is much higher from the rest of the area. When looking at the satellite map, one can see that it is because Nijmegen is built on hills. Below that map, one can see the digital terrain map, where the map focuses on how high the whole area is, including trees and buildings. This helps to concentrate the map more and only focus on the elevation of everything. It is even more clear how high Nijmegen lies. A downside of this type of map is that the villages and city are not as clearly visible.

Furthermore, one can see the newer map overlaid on top of the historic map from 1843. The river has changed, even though it has not even been two centuries. There are some slight changes in the waters around the river as well. Due to the lightness of the map below, some differences are hard to see however. On the right bottom, the old map used can be seen. It is a small part of the bigger map by W.Kuyk etc.

Reflection

A few of the new skills I learned include learning to layer two maps on top of another to be able to analyze them both. Furthermore, to play around with shadow layers, in order to help the visibility of the maps. To clip the maps to the wanted size, or to get rid of some unwanted parts was also new for me. Overlaying two maps with coordinates to make them match perfectly on top of each other was a nice technique to learn as well.

The old map I chose for it did not have very clear black lines, which makes the differences within the map somewhat hard to read. In the next version, a map with clearer lines should be chosen, to clearly see the differences between the older and newer map. A map like this can be used for various reasons. The DSM map with elevation shows the region clearly and what areas are in danger of flooding, what experts could use for the next dikes. For the general public, or people living in the area it is also interesting, as they can learn about their region.
